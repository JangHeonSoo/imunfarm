# South Korea's 'Energy Great Transformation': Renewable Energy Target of 20% by 2030

---

It started with the Middle East.

On April 6, 2026, Minister Kim Sung-hwan of the Ministry of Climate, Energy and Environment presented the 'People's Sovereignty Government Energy Transformation Plan' to the cabinet meeting. The declaration: overhaul the nation's fossil-fuel-dependent energy structure from the ground up. The central goal — raise the share of renewable energy in power generation from the current ~9% to over 20% by 2030.

This is not just a number on a policy chart. It is the largest energy restructuring experiment South Korea has ever attempted.

---

## Where the Numbers Stand Today

The starting line is not impressive.

South Korea's renewable energy share in total power generation stood at 8.6% in 2024 — near the bottom among IEA member countries, where the average is 33% and Japan clocks in at 24%. 2025 marked the first year renewables crossed the 10% threshold, a symbolic milestone, but the gap to 20% remains steep.

Current installed capacity sits at roughly 37GW. The plan calls for nearly tripling that to 100GW by 2030. In 2025 alone, 3.8GW of new renewable capacity came online — to meet the 2030 target, Korea needs to sustain nearly twice that pace every year for the next four years.

---

## What the Plan Actually Contains

The framework is built around 3 policy directions and 10 tasks.

### Expanding Renewables: Solar and Wind Across All Terrain

The core strategy is diversifying installation sites. Rooftop solar on industrial complexes, agrivoltaic systems layered over farmland, and floating solar on reservoirs — every usable space becomes a candidate.

By 2026: solar panels installed at 50+ traditional markets, 1,500+ parking lots, and 500 schools. By 2030: 6,400 schools covered. The renewable energy budget has been raised from KRW 1.1 trillion to KRW 1.5 trillion for 2026.

For wind, the bottleneck has always been permitting. Multi-year approval processes are to be compressed through integrated site designation and batch permitting for offshore wind.

### Coal Phase-Out

All 60 operational coal-fired power plants are slated for sequential retirement by 2040, with a formal roadmap to be established shortly. A special law will be enacted to support affected regions — fostering replacement industries and cushioning displaced workers through what the government calls a "just transition".

### Green Manufacturing Powerhouse

The plan targets becoming one of the world's top 3 nations in green manufacturing — covering solar panels, wind turbines, and BESS (Battery Energy Storage Systems). Hydrogen-based steelmaking and electrified petrochemical processes are also on the roadmap. By 2035, the plan is to have shifted the backbone of Korea's export competitiveness toward renewable-energy-based industry.

### Energy Income for Citizens

The 'Sunshine Income Village' program stands out. It is a resident-participation model in which local communities invest in solar and wind projects and share in the revenue. Starting with 500 villages in 2026 and expanding to 2,500 by 2030, the goal is to make 10 million citizens — roughly 1 in 5 Koreans — active participants in the energy transition.

---

## The Biggest Obstacle: The Grid

The ambition is real. But the most immediate barrier is unglamorous: not enough power lines.

Over the past decade, renewable energy capacity in Korea grew by 495% — while actual generation increased only 230%. The gap points directly at grid congestion. In just the first half of 2025, approximately 164GWh of electricity could not be delivered due to insufficient transmission infrastructure. That is equivalent to six months of electricity consumption for about 97,000 households.

Between 2013 and 2023, transmission infrastructure grew by only 14% and distribution networks by 22% — nowhere near the pace of capacity additions. More than half of 54 major planned grid projects are already delayed.

A geographic mismatch compounds the problem: renewable generation is concentrated in the southern regions — South and North Jeolla provinces — while demand surges in the Seoul metropolitan area driven by semiconductor clusters and AI data centers. Electricity cannot reach where it's needed because the wires aren't there.

Building a major transmission line takes more than 8 years from start to finish. For a 2030 target, the shovels needed to go in the ground yesterday.

The government's answer is the 'Energy Highway' initiative: AI-managed smart grid operations, 2.3GW of ESS rollout by 2029, and virtual power plant (VPP) activation. Whether execution can match ambition — that is the question every industry observer is asking.

---

## Economic Stakes

The economic rationale is clear.

Shifting public export finance from fossil fuels to renewables could more than double domestic job creation. A Greenpeace analysis estimated that energy transition investments could generate 810,000–860,000 jobs between 2022 and 2030 alone.

The RE100 supply chain pressure is equally concrete. Korea's 36 RE100 member companies consume 68TWh of electricity annually but source only 12% from renewables on average. Major exporters like Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix face mounting pressure from European and American customers demanding higher clean energy usage. Energy transition is a climate issue, yes — but it is also a competitiveness issue.

---

## Structural Headwinds

Speed is the plan. But several structural brakes are applied simultaneously.

KEPCO's centralized monopoly over the transmission network limits private investment flows — when KEPCO faces financial difficulties, grid expansion stalls. Some analysts warn that draft provisions in the Gwangju-Jeonnam Special Act — which would allow KEPCO to directly enter renewable generation — could actually entrench rather than reform the existing structure.

Community acceptance remains a perennial challenge. Opposition to transmission line construction and renewable siting near residential areas continues to slow project timelines. The 'Sunshine Income Village' model is partly a response to this: make communities stakeholders, not bystanders.

## This Direction Was Always Coming

Rewind to 2017. The 'Renewable Energy 3020 Implementation Plan' already set a target of 20% renewables by 2030. Subsequent administrations slowed the pace. The current government, inaugurated in 2025, re-established the Ministry of Climate, Energy and Environment and declared 2026 the 'Year of Energy Transformation Results'.

The immediate catalyst was geopolitical: the Israel-Iran conflict sharpened the case for reducing oil import dependency. That vulnerability — Korea importing most of its energy from politically unstable regions — was the argument the government used to rally public support for structural change.

The declaration to end Middle Eastern energy dependence is therefore not environmental rhetoric alone. It is a convergence point where energy security, export competitiveness, and climate commitment all point in the same direction.
